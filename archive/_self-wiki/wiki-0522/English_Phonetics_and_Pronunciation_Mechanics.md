### Distillation (2026-05-20) - source: [[../raw/diary/learning/2016-10-04-english-listening-and-pronunciation.md]]
**Core Principles Ingested:**
1. **Rhythm and Stress:** English is a beat-driven, stress-timed language. Focus must be placed on content words (stressed) versus function words (de-stressed).
2. **Prosody (Pitch & Intonation):** Pitch variation must be used strategically: raising pitch on stressed words and allowing the sentence to fall at the end for declarative statements, or rising for questions/uncertainty.
3. **Articulatory Detail:** Specific sounds (e.g., /θ/, /ð/, /r/) require precise muscle memory exercises. For instance, the difference between /i/ (relaxed jaw) and /i:/ (tongue stretches high) is critical.
4. **Linking and Reduction:** Phenomena like Schwa reduction (/ə/) and vowel-to-vowel linking (/i:, ai, ei/ -> /j/) are essential for achieving natural flow and must be practiced through shadowing.

**Actionable Takeaway:** Mastery requires moving from theoretical knowledge (IPA) to embodied practice (mouth movement, repetition, and auditory imitation).

**## Core Principles of Fluency**
*   **Holistic Focus**: Shift focus from individual sounds to the overall 'feeling' and rhythm of the sentence. 
*   **Pitch & Intonation**: Use pitch variation (raised for stress words, dropped at sentence end) to convey meaning. Intonation acts as a GPS for the listener.
*   **Stress**: Apply stress through volume, length, and pitch. Function words often undergo 'de-stress' (relaxed and short).

**## Connected Speech Mechanics (Reduction & Linking)**
*   **Reduction**: Observe how unstressed syllables reduce (e.g., 'to' $\rightarrow$ /tə/, 'and' $\rightarrow$ /ən/).
*   **Linking/Elision**: Study how sounds blend (e.g., /t/ + /j/ $\rightarrow$ /tʃ/).

**## Practice Protocols**
*   **Shadowing**: Mimic native speakers to internalize rhythm and sound patterns.
*   **Active Listening**: Write down what you hear to train auditory discrimination.
