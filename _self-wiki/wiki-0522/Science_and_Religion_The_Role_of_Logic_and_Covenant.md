> This text draws a parallel between the development of ancient Israelite religion and the emergence of formal logic and mathematics. It posits that the absolute covenant between God and the people necessitated a rigorous, uncompromising logic capable of sustaining the concept of absolute truth, leading to the development of contradiction and excluded middle principles. The author contrasts this with ancient Chinese thought, which, while valuing debate, lacked the absolute, life-or-death stakes required to push logic to the level of formal systems.



### Distillation (2026-05-26) - source: [[../raw/diary/2014-04-04-math-is-the-basis-of-science.md]]
**The Covenant and Logic:** The foundation of ancient Israelite religion was a covenant with an absolute God, demanding absolute adherence. This absolute requirement drove the need for a rigorous, uncompromising logical system capable of testing and maintaining that covenant's truth. This necessity birthed the formal logic that underpins mathematics.

**The Limits of Chinese Thought:** While ancient China valued logical debate, its disputes were often between equals, dependent on persuasion and mutual understanding, lacking the absolute, life-or-death stakes of the divine covenant. This context favored pragmatic, subjective debate over the development of objective, formal logical structures.

**The Birth of Formal Logic:** The absolute nature of the covenant demanded adherence to absolute logical laws: the Law of Contradiction (no two propositions can be simultaneously true and false) and the Law of Excluded Middle (a proposition must be either true or false).

**Synthesis with Modernity:** Modern mathematics relies on this formal logic, which is rooted in the absolute demands of the covenant. The author also touches upon the modern capitalist system, whose foundation in absolute private property rights mirrors this need for uncompromising logical frameworks, contrasting this with the fluidity of pre-modern systems.

**Induction vs. Deduction:** The text distinguishes between the inductive method (used in empirical science, which only yields 'possibly correct' conclusions) and the deductive method (theoretically capable of absolute truth, as seen in pure mathematics).
