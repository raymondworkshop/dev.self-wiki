**Human Language as Symbolic System:** A human language functions as a symbolic signaling system where words act as signifiers mapping to signified concepts. The continuous encoding of these signals relates to deep cognitive problems, such as the nature of thought itself.

**Linguistic Components:**
- **Phonetics/Phonology:** Study of sound patterns, including features like word stress (achieved via pitch, volume, or duration) and intonation, which can carry syntactic or semantic weight.
- **Morphology:** Rules governing word formation, utilizing morphemes as the smallest units of meaning.
- **Syntax/Semantics/Pragmatics:** Cover sentence structure, inherent linguistic meaning, and how context modifies that meaning, respectively.

**Computational Approaches:**
- **Word Vectors/Embeddings:** Produce dense vector representations based on context. They encode semantic information, relying on the distributional hypothesis: similar words appear in similar contexts (e.g., Word2Vec).
- **Language Modeling:** Treats language as a probabilistic model of word sequences, historically modeled by N-grams, and more recently by RNNs/LSTMs.

**Cognitive Challenge:** The exploration of continuous encoding signals bridges cognitive science, psychology, and computational theory, touching upon fundamental challenges like the Turing Test.
