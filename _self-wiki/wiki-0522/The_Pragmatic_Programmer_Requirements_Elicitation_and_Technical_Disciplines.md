> This document synthesizes core tenets from 'The Pragmatic Programmer,' focusing on requirements engineering and technical discipline. It emphasizes that requirements must capture the underlying business need (the 'why'), while specific implementations are documented as policy, and that plain text is the most persistent medium for knowledge.



### Distillation (2026-05-20) - source: [[../raw/diary/notes/2018-05-11-the-pragmatic-programming.md]]
**Requirements Elicitation (Ch7 Before the project)**
*   **General Statement vs. Policy:** The requirement must be the general statement reflecting the business need; specific implementation details serve as policy examples.
*   **Root Cause Analysis:** Focus on discovering the underlying reason ('why') behind user actions, not just the surface-level 'how.'
*   **Use Cases:** Proposing use cases provides a common ground for discussion among developers, end-users, and sponsors.
*   **Abstraction:** Requirements must capture the underlying semantic invariants; abstractions endure longer than transient details.

**Technical Disciplines & Tools**
*   **Plain Text as Base Material:** Plain text is the optimal format for persistently storing knowledge, as our base material is knowledge itself.
*   **Power Editing:** Mastery of a single editor for all text manipulation tasks is crucial for efficiency.
*   **Debugging:** Debugging is fundamentally problem-solving. Attack the problem, not the blame. This requires visualizing data, tracing state over time, and proving assumptions.

**Traceability Note:** All concepts are drawn from *The Pragmatic Programmer* by Andrew Hunt & David Thomas.
