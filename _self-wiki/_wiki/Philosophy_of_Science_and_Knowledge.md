It explores the tension between theory-driven deduction and observation-driven induction, mapping these concepts onto broader philosophical frameworks.

## Content

### The Scientific Process: From Confirmation to Falsification

The modern scientific journey is not a linear path from certainty to discovery, but rather a dynamic process of **trial and error**.
> To move forward, **science needs to fail**, which runs counter to our human desire for certainty. The journey to understand the mysteries of the world and explain them with reason is the core of our nature. (Source: [[self-wiki/raw/diary/learning/2022-01-24-notes-on-philosophy-and-thinking.md]])

#### **Falsifiability (Popperian View)**
-   **The Shift:** Modern science moves from *finding the correct answer* to *advancing through attempts and refutations*.
-   **The Reality:** What is considered correct today might be proven wrong tomorrow.
-   **The Method:** Science progresses not by proving theories right, but by attempting to prove them wrong.

#### **Epistemological Approaches**

**1. Rationalism (先验理性)**
-   **Basis:** Knowledge derived from innate ideas, pure reason, and deductive logic, independent of sensory experience.
-   **Analogy:** Looking at the world from a perfect, detached vantage point.
-   **Strength:** Provides the most coherent, logically sound theoretical structures.
-   **Weakness:** Can become detached from messy reality if not grounded.

**2. Empiricism (经验理性)**
-   **Basis:** Knowledge derived from sensory experience and observation.
-   **Method:** **Inductive Logic**—forming generalizations from specific observations.
-   **Strength:** Highly adaptive and grounded in observable reality, making it crucial for practical application (e.g., common law).
-   **Weakness:** The leap from specific instances to universal laws is always probabilistic, not certain.

**3. Synthesis: The Scientific Method**
-   **The Cycle:** The most robust approach integrates both:
    1.  **Observation/Data Collection (Empirical Input):** Start with observed phenomena (the "fragments").
    2.  **Hypothesis Generation (Conceptual Framework):** Develop a working model or theory to explain the fragments.
    3.  **Deduction/Prediction (Rational Application):** Use the framework to predict future observations.
    4.  **Testing/Falsification (Empirical Test):** Seek out observations that contradict the prediction.
    5.  **Refinement:** If contradicted, revise the framework; if supported, strengthen the theory.
-   **The Danger of Framework Rigidity:** A premature, overly confident framework can lead to confirmation bias, causing practitioners to ignore contradictory evidence ("dogmatism").

### Metaphysics and Application

-   **Free Market vs. Planned Economy:**
    -   Free Market: Dynamic equilibrium, relies on post-hoc correction.
    -   Planned Economy: Static equilibrium, relies on upfront planning.
-   **Morality:** Extreme ideologies often fail because they jump directly from a phenomenon to a moral answer, ignoring the complex causal chain. Sound judgment requires rigorous testing of the *reason* behind the claim, not just its moral purity.

## Cognitive Dimensions

-   **Cognitive Intelligence:** Tends toward analytical, deductive reasoning (the "thinking").
-   **Emotional Intelligence:** The capacity to accurately perceive and navigate emotions and motives (the "feeling").
-   **Integration:** True effectiveness requires balancing rigorous analysis with deep emotional context.
