> Stoicism is an ancient philosophy offering frameworks to navigate life's inevitable difficulties toward greater peace and happiness. Its core tenets revolve around mastering one's internal responses, directing actions virtuously, and accepting what lies beyond personal influence.

## Content

### Core Tenets of Stoicism
Stoicism, founded by Zeno of Citium, provides a path to achieving tranquility by focusing on internal mastery:
1. **Control Perceptions**: Manage how you interpret events.
2. **Direct Actions Properly**: Ensure your actions align with virtue and reason.
3. **Accept the Uncontrollable**: Voluntarily release attachment to outcomes outside your sphere of influence.

### Advanced Applications (Ch2 & Ch3)

#### 1. Negative Visualization (消極想像)
*   **Practice**: Regularly contemplate the loss of valued possessions or states of being.
*   **Benefit**: This practice fosters appreciation for what is currently possessed, grounding joy in the present reality, while simultaneously preparing the mind for potential loss.
*   **Outcome**: It cultivates emotional freedom by decoupling happiness from transient circumstances.
*   **Application**: One must strive diligently and care deeply, but remain mentally prepared to let go of the final outcome, viewing failure as a learning experience for the next attempt.

#### 2. Dichotomy of Control (区分能控制与不能控制)
When facing daily matters, categorize elements into three groups:
*   **Fully Controllable**: Your goals and values. Focus energy here, as correct choices here yield massive returns.
*   **Partially Controllable**: Matters where you can exert effort but not guarantee the outcome. Set *internal* goals here (e.g., "I will try my absolute best"), thus mitigating external disappointment.
*   **Uncontrollable**: External events. Place these aside without worry.

#### 3. Living in the Present (活在當下)
*   The past cannot be altered; the present moment is the only actionable domain.

#### 4. Social Duty and Relationships (熱愛人類是我們的義務)
*   Human existence inherently involves relationships; fulfilling this social duty is part of being human.
*   **Pragmatic View**: While relationships are often transactional, one must engage with difficult individuals for the greater good.
*   **Discernment**: Distinguish between necessary, transactional interactions and deep, mutually beneficial partnerships to conserve vital energy.

#### 5. Navigating Conflict and Insults (侮辱)
*   **Deconstruct the Insult**: Question the veracity of the criticism. Is it factually true, or merely the speaker's subjective feeling?
*   **Source Analysis**: The true source of distress often lies in one's *reaction* to the event, not the event itself.
*   **Response Strategies**:
    *   **Humor**: Self-deprecation can neutralize aggression.
    *   **Silence**: Sometimes, no response is the most powerful action.
    *   **Correction**: If the speaker is in a position of authority (e.g., a paid teacher), address the flawed premise calmly.

#### 6. Managing Grief and Anger (悲痛 & 憤怒)
*   **Grief**: Use negative visualization to temper the blow of loss. Reason is the antidote to overwhelming emotion; it helps filter out the excessive parts of sorrow.
*   **Anger**: Recognize that allowing minor irritations to disrupt inner peace is foolish. Life is transient; do not waste it in wrath.

#### 7. Reputation (名聲)
*   True self-possession allows one to be unconcerned with external judgment. Seeking acceptance from others often means sacrificing one's authentic vision.
