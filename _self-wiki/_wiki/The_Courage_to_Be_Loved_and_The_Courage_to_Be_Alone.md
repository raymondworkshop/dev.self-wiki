> This document synthesizes Alfred Adler's concepts regarding the courage required for individual autonomy ("doing oneself") and the courage required for genuine happiness ("loving"). It posits that true happiness arises from a shift from egocentrism to a commitment to the "we" through mutual respect and contribution to a shared community.

## Evolution
- Initial concepts regarding Adlerian psychology are being integrated into a structured framework for personal application, moving from theoretical summary to actionable life philosophy.

## Content

### I. The Courage to Be Independent (做自己)
The foundation of self-actualization lies in the courage to define oneself independently, without seeking validation from others.
- **Autonomy of Self:** One must choose their path as a "lifestyle," not merely a career, and accept that one is not living to meet others' expectations, nor are others living to meet yours.
- **Freedom from Judgment:** One must not fear the gaze or judgment of others, nor seek their approval; the path chosen must be authentically one's own.
- **Separation of Tasks (课题分离):** This requires not interfering in the tasks of others, nor allowing others to interfere in one's own.

### II. The Courage to Find Happiness (获得幸福)
True, lasting happiness is found not in isolation but within a "community" guided by mutual commitment.
- **The Role of Love:** Happiness is achieved through "Love"—a mutual support system characterized by heartfelt respect, genuine care, and shared interest as independent individuals, coupled with the willing risk of a social community.
- **Commitment to the Journey:** Happiness is not a destination to be "learned"; it is a path that must be actively "engaged in." It requires relentless forward effort and courage.
- **Attitude Towards Adversity:** One must approach the world with love rather than hate, as hate is easier but leads to mutual destruction. The focus must always be on "What to do next," rather than dwelling on victimhood.

### III. The Dynamics of Relationships (人际关系)
Relationships are the crucible where autonomy meets connection.
- **Work vs. Friendship:**
    - **Work Relationships:** Are transactional, based on external factors, and require trust in competence for the task at hand.
    - **Friendship Relationships:** Are voluntary, based on intrinsic liking of the person themselves, and are the foundation for genuine belonging.
- **The Primacy of Respect (尊重):** All relationships begin and are sustained by respect. Respect is defined as "seeing a person as they are" and acknowledging their unique individuality without attempting to control or mold them. This acceptance is the root of courage.
- **The Shift from Ego to We:** Love requires shedding egocentrism. The pursuit of happiness must shift from "My happiness" to "Our shared happiness," where the self dissolves into the collective contribution.

### IV. Philosophical Underpinnings (哲学层面)
- **Philosophy vs. Religion vs. Science:** Science deals with objective facts; philosophy and religion delve into the "ultimate truths" of the human spirit. The difference lies in narrative: religion offers a story with protagonists, while philosophy uses abstract concepts without a fixed protagonist.
- **The Pursuit of Truth:** Like the pursuit of truth, philosophy is an unending climb up the "staff of unknowing." To claim to have reached the end is to enter religion and cease growing.

## Conclusion
The journey toward a meaningful existence requires the courage to be fully oneself (autonomy) while simultaneously committing to the vulnerability of loving another (connection). This balance—the ability to be self-reliant while choosing to contribute to a shared "we"—is the definition of a life lived with courage and finding happiness.
