> Travel is portrayed not merely as seeing new places, but as a journey inward, forcing a confrontation with one's own ingrained concepts and biases. Through experiencing the unfamiliar, one gains a richer, more nuanced view of both the external world and the internal self. This journey is described as a necessary "love affair" with the unknown.

## Evolution
- Initial thoughts on travel were anecdotal, rooted in a personal memory of Xinjiang and the initial pull of the unknown.
- The reflection evolved by integrating philosophical quotes (Picasso, Twain, Proust) to elevate the personal experience into a universal cognitive model.
- The current synthesis frames travel as a rigorous intellectual and emotional exercise in cognitive deconstruction and reconstruction.

## Content
The act of traveling serves as a powerful catalyst for personal growth, moving beyond mere sightseeing to become a profound encounter with the self. The initial attraction to travel stems from a fundamental human curiosity about the world and the limits of one's own knowledge.

**The Journey of Discovery:**
The author recounts the transformative experience of traveling to Yunnan and Xinjiang, where the vastness of the landscapes (grasslands, deserts, glaciers) provided a visceral awakening. This initial encounter with the sublime—like the clear morning at Sayram Lake—is unforgettable and fuels a continuous drive to explore.

**Philosophical Dimensions of Travel:**
1. **Picasso's View:** Travel begins with the desire to lose oneself, but ultimately culminates in the discovery of the true self. It is an experience of feeling and living.
2. **The Need for Expansion:** To truly understand the world, one must move beyond mediated knowledge. Travel allows for the direct apprehension of diverse geographies, cultures, and human experiences.
3. **Twain's Tenet:** The most crucial belief is continuous growth; one must constantly change until the end of life. Travel facilitates this by pushing the boundaries of vision and spirit.
4. **The Rigor of Experience:** The author frames travel as a "self-inflicted hardship," willingly seeking difficulty to experience both one's own struggles and the hardships of others, achieving a balance between objective observation and genuine feeling.
5. **Proust's Insight:** True exploration is not about seeing new places, but about viewing old realities through new lenses. The journey forces the deconstruction of deeply held, unquestioned concepts ("taken for granted") that prove inadequate when confronted with different contexts.

**Conclusion: The Love Affair:**
Ultimately, each journey is described as a "love affair." When one approaches a place or people with openness, the distance dissolves. The land and its inhabitants become internalized memories, residing within the traveler's consciousness, making the experience deeply personal and enduring.
