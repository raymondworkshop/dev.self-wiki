> The learning process benefits from a dynamic approach: starting with fragments and building toward a framework, rather than imposing a rigid framework prematurely. A fixed framework risks ossification and inability to adapt to new information, whereas a fragment-based approach allows for continuous revision and eventual superior synthesis.

## Evolution
- Initial thoughts suggested that a premature framework leads to rigidity and inability to incorporate new data, causing the system to become brittle.
- The current synthesis advocates for a fluid methodology: fragments first, then framework construction, and constant revision of that framework.

## Content
### The Pitfalls of Premature Frameworks
A framework established too early, while providing initial coherence and apparent advanced understanding, carries a significant risk:
- **Rigidity and Dogmatism**: Once solidified, the framework can become an unquestioned dogma, leading to intellectual inflexibility.
- **Tunnel Vision**: It can lead to "tunnel vision," causing one to disproportionately value small gains while missing larger, paradigm-shifting truths ("占小便宜吃大亏").

### The Power of Fragment-Based Learning
The superior path involves:
- **Starting with Fragments**: Beginning with disparate pieces of information allows for a broader intake of data without the constraint of a premature model.
- **Iterative Construction**: These fragments are then continuously assembled, tested, and used to build, refine, or even entirely overthrow the initial conceptual framework.
- **Achieving Depth**: While this process appears slower initially, it ultimately leads to a more robust and adaptable understanding ("后来局上").
