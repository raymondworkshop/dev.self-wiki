> This document synthesizes frameworks for distinguishing between "difficult problems" requiring novel cognitive input and "hard work" requiring sustained effort. The core principle is to reduce the cognitive cost of decision-making by collapsing high-dimensional problem spaces into manageable, low-dimensional tasks.

## Evolution
- Initial concepts were drawn from disparate sources regarding problem-solving methodologies. This synthesis integrates the distinction between the *nature* of the problem (ambiguity vs. routine) and the *process* of solving it (cognitive leap vs. execution).

## Content

### Defining the Problem Space
**Difficult Problems**
These are problems that cannot be solved merely through the expenditure of human hours; they fundamentally require dealing with **ambiguity** and a significant injection of **skill or novel thinking**.
> (Source: [[self-wiki/raw/diary/notes/2021-03-01-how-to-work-on-hard-things.md]])

**Hard Work**
Hard work, in contrast, is often defined as the sheer accumulation of human hours applied to a task.
> (Source: [[self-wiki/raw/diary/notes/2021-03-01-how-to-work-on-hard-things.md]])

### Effective Strategies for Difficult Problems
To tackle truly difficult problems, the focus must shift from brute force to cognitive leverage:

1.  **Reduce Cognitive Costs:** The primary strategy is to reduce the cognitive load associated with decision-making. This involves collapsing a high-dimensional problem space into a low-dimensional execution task.
    *   Focus only on decisions where *anything* you can do is viable.
    *   Prioritize decisions that offer the quickest path to a positive outcome.
    *   When necessary, delegate the decision-making process to external experts or sources.
    > (Source: [[self-wiki/raw/diary/notes/2021-03-01-how-to-work-on-hard-things.md]])

2.  **Iterative Refinement (The Feedback Loop):**
    *   **Start Simple, Then Evolve:** Always begin by implementing the simplest solution that addresses the immediate issue. This solution must be viewed as provisional and subject to change when deeper understanding emerges.
    *   **Deeply Understand the Problem:** After implementing a simple solution, the true understanding of the problem often becomes clearer. Therefore, one must constantly question: "What is the problem I am trying to solve?" and "How will I know when it is solved?"
    *   **Validate the Action:** Before proceeding, rigorously check: "Does this action solve my problem?" and "Is this action necessary for the ultimate goal?"

3.  **Skill Acquisition:** For problems requiring high levels of skill, accessing a subject matter expert and following their guidance is a valid and efficient strategy.

### Guiding Principles
*   **Focus on the Matters:** Not every issue encountered in life requires a grand, complex solution; coexistence with the problem is often a normal part of existence.
*   **Creativity as Investment:** Apply creativity, skills, and effort toward solving large problems, expecting the market (or context) to provide compensation for that unique contribution. If the work does not demand creativity, it should be delegated, automated, or discarded.
