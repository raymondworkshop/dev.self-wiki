Antifragility is a concept introduced by Nassim Nicholas Taleb, describing things that benefit from shocks, volatility, and disorder. It goes beyond resilience or robustness, which merely resist shocks, by actually improving because of them.

### The Triad: Fragile, Robust, Antifragile

- **Fragile**: Wants tranquility, fears disorder. It breaks under stress (e.g., a glass vase).
- **Robust**: Withstands shocks without changing (e.g., a phoenix, though better seen as just resilient).
- **Antifragile**: Gains from disorder. Shocks and stressors make it stronger (e.g., the Hydra, evolution, a startup ecosystem).

### Core Concepts

#### Optionality
The key to antifragility is having options. If you have options, you don't need to be right about the future; you just need the ability to pivot when things change. 
*Strategy*: Maximize asymmetric bets where the downside is limited and the upside is open-ended.

#### Via Negativa
Improving systems by removing things (subtraction) rather than adding more complexity.
- Focus on what to *stop* doing.
- Eliminating fragile elements makes the overall system more robust or antifragile.

#### Lindy Effect
The idea that the future life expectancy of non-perishable things (like ideas, books, or technologies) is proportional to their current age. 
*Application*: Favor old, time-tested wisdom (like [[Stoicism]]) over the latest trends.

### Personal and Organizational Antifragility
- **Skin in the Game**: True learning and accountability only happen when one has a personal stake in the outcome.
- **Small Failures**: Systems that allow for frequent, small-scale failures (like a market or the scientific process) avoid the catastrophic "black swan" events that destroy fragile systems.
- **Self-Management**: Developing an "inner citadel" (as in [[Stoicism]]) makes the individual antifragile to external social and economic shifts.
