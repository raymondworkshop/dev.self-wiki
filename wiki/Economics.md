Economics is the study of resource allocation, competition, and the institutional frameworks that facilitate exchange. It emphasizes the roles of private property rights and transaction costs in creating efficient markets, alongside practical applications in personal wealth management and financial security.

### Property Rights and Markets
- **Private Property Rights**: The backbone of capitalism. Without clearly defined private property, true market prices cannot exist, leading to resource waste.
- **Market Value as a Criterion**: In any economic system, "survival of the fittest" applies. Using market value as the criterion for competition minimizes waste compared to other social or political criteria.
- **Capitalism and Ownership**: Modern capitalism is rooted in the concept of absolute and abstract ownership, often traced back to religious concepts of the Creator's rights over creation.

### Transaction Costs and Organizations
- **The Coase Theorem**: Organizations (like firms) exist because they can perform certain transactions more cheaply than the market.
- **Transaction Costs**: These include information costs, bargaining costs, and enforcement costs. When these costs are high, markets may fail to determine efficient prices.
- **Democracy and Markets**: Markets can be seen as highly democratic "voting" systems where "votes" (money) reflect the cost and intensity of preference, unlike political voting which can lead to inefficient wealth redistribution.

### Economic Logic and Management
- **Number-based Management**: Modern economies rely on the ability to manage at scale using "numbers" (mathematically manageable systems), requiring internal regularization of law and commerce.
- **Rule of Law vs. Moral Governance**: Economic efficiency increases when systems move from discretionary moral judgment to predictable legal and technical frameworks.

### Personal Financial Strategy
Effective wealth management integrates economic principles with structured personal habits:
- **Asset Allocation**: A robust strategy balances active and passive income streams, typically prioritizing growth-oriented stocks (e.g., 50-60%) alongside index ETFs and retirement funds. Regular rebalancing is essential.
- **Cash Flow and Security**:
    - **Emergency Funds**: Essential for maintaining liquidity without needing to liquidate long-term investments.
    - **Insurance Principles**: Risk management is fundamental, utilizing health, life, and disability insurance to cover basic risks, allowing for more aggressive investment in growth sectors.
- **Disciplined Execution**: Investment is not merely passive; it requires managing emotions (avoiding FOMO or panic selling), maintaining discipline, and setting clear stop-loss/take-profit targets to avoid ruinous "彌補損失" (trying to recoup losses) behaviors.
- **Long-term Goal**: Systematic saving (e.g., aiming for a specific target by age 65) benefits from economic growth and compound interest, provided the financial environment is diversified.
